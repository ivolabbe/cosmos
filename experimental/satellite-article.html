Satellite

A satellite is any object that orbits another, more massive body. Natural satellites include the Moon, which orbits the Earth, and the many moons of the outer planets. Since the launch of Sputnik 1 in 1957, the term has become closely associated with artificial satellites — spacecraft placed into orbit around the Earth or other bodies for communication, navigation, observation and scientific research.

As of early 2026, more than 10,000 active artificial satellites orbit the Earth, the vast majority in low Earth orbit. Their orbital paths are governed by the same gravitational principles described by Kepler’s laws that determine the motion of natural celestial bodies.

Types of orbit
Satellites occupy a range of orbits depending on their purpose. These are characterised by their altitude, inclination and eccentricity.

  • Low Earth Orbit (LEO) — below 2,000 km altitude, with orbital periods of roughly 90 minutes at speeds of ~7.8 km/s. LEO hosts the International Space Station, Earth-observation missions, and large constellations such as Starlink. Orbital planes are distributed across all inclinations.
  • Medium Earth Orbit (MEO) — between 2,000 and 35,786 km. Navigation constellations occupy this regime: GPS (USA), GLONASS (Russia), and Galileo (Europe) each operate in a small number of evenly-spaced orbital planes at inclinations of 55–65°.
  • Geostationary Orbit (GEO) — a circular orbit at exactly 35,786 km altitude in the equatorial plane. At this distance, a satellite’s period matches the Earth’s rotation (23 h 56 m), so it appears stationary from the ground. Three evenly-spaced GEO satellites can provide near-global coverage, making this orbit ideal for telecommunications and weather monitoring.
  • Highly Elliptical Orbit (HEO) — orbits with high eccentricity. A satellite in a Molniya orbit, for example, spends most of its 12-hour period at high altitude over high-latitude regions, providing coverage where GEO satellites cannot reach.

Specialised variants include polar orbits, which pass over both poles and allow full-globe coverage as the Earth rotates beneath, and sun-synchronous orbits (600–800 km), which maintain a fixed orientation relative to the Sun, ensuring consistent lighting conditions for Earth-observation imagery.

The space environment
The region around the Earth is increasingly congested. In addition to active satellites, approximately 36,500 pieces of debris larger than 10 cm are tracked by radar, an estimated one million fragments between 1 and 10 cm are too small to track individually, and more than 100 million particles smaller than 1 cm orbit the Earth. Collisions, such as the 2009 Iridium–Cosmos event, can generate thousands of new fragments, raising the risk of a cascading debris environment known as the Kessler syndrome. Active debris mitigation and end-of-life de-orbiting are now standard requirements for new missions.
