Zenith

The zenith is the point on the celestial sphere located directly above an observer. In the horizontal coordinate system, the zenith has an altitude of +90° and an undefined azimuth. Its opposite point—the point directly below the observer—is the nadir, at an altitude of −90°. Together, the zenith and nadir define the vertical axis of the observer’s local sky.

The observer’s horizon — The plane perpendicular to the zenith–nadir line at the observer’s location defines the local horizon. Objects above this plane are visible (altitude > 0°), while objects below it are hidden from view. The horizon thus divides the celestial sphere into a visible hemisphere and an invisible one.

The celestial meridian — The celestial meridian is the great circle on the celestial sphere that passes through the zenith, the nadir, and both celestial poles. The upper meridian—the half above the horizon—runs from the north point on the horizon through the zenith to the south point. When a star crosses the upper meridian, it undergoes a meridian transit and reaches its greatest altitude for that day, a moment known as upper culmination.

Celestial poles and latitude — The celestial poles are the projections of the Earth’s rotation axis onto the celestial sphere. A fundamental relationship links the observer’s geographic latitude to the altitude of the visible celestial pole: the pole stands at an altitude equal to the observer’s latitude. For an observer in Melbourne at latitude −38°, the south celestial pole sits 38° above the southern horizon, and the zenith lies 38° from that pole. In contrast, an observer at the equator sees the zenith on the celestial equator itself, with both poles resting on the horizon.

Celestial equator and the sky — The celestial equator—the projection of Earth’s equator onto the celestial sphere—intersects the observer’s horizon at the exact east and west points, regardless of latitude. For a mid-latitude observer, the celestial equator traces an arc across the sky that passes through the zenith only at the geographic equator. The declination of the zenith at any moment equals the observer’s latitude, a fact that historically made zenith observations essential for determining geographic position.

Circumpolar stars — Because the celestial sphere appears to rotate daily about the celestial poles, stars near the visible pole never set—they are circumpolar. For an observer at latitude φ, any star with a declination within |φ|° of the visible pole remains above the horizon at all times. Conversely, stars within the same angular distance of the opposite pole never rise. The remaining stars rise in the east, reach their highest altitude as they cross the upper meridian, and set in the west.

The zenith and the network of reference circles it defines—horizon, meridian, celestial equator—form the geometric foundation of the horizontal coordinate system, linking every position on the sky to the observer’s location on Earth.
