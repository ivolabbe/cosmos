Binary Star

A binary star is a system of two stars that are gravitationally bound and orbit their common centre of mass. Binary systems are remarkably common: surveys show that approximately 50% of solar-type stars have at least one stellar companion, and the fraction rises for more massive stars (Duchêne & Kraus 2013). The two components obey Kepler’s laws of motion, tracing elliptical or circular orbits with periods ranging from under a day to thousands of years. Because binary stars allow direct measurement of stellar masses and radii, they are among the most important objects in all of astronomy.

Types of Binary Stars

Binary systems are classified by the method used to detect them. The same physical system may fall into more than one category depending on its orientation relative to the observer and the instruments available.

Type Observable What it reveals
Visual Both stars resolved on the sky Orbital period, angular semi-major axis; with parallax, true masses
SB1 (single-lined spectroscopic) One set of spectral lines shifting K1, P, e → mass function f(M)
SB2 (double-lined spectroscopic) Two sets of shifting lines K1, K2 → mass ratio + individual M sin3(i)
Eclipsing Periodic brightness dips Inclination i ≈ 90°; combined with radial velocity data → absolute masses and radii
Astrometric Wobble in position on the sky Companion mass from wobble amplitude, after accounting for proper motion and parallax

Classification after Hilditch (2001), An Introduction to Close Binary Stars.

Orbital Mechanics

The motion of a binary system is a two-body problem that reduces to an equivalent one-body problem in the centre-of-mass frame. Each star orbits the common centre of mass at a distance inversely proportional to its mass: if the total separation is a, then star 1 orbits at a1 = a M2 / (M1 + M2) and star 2 at a2 = a M1 / (M1 + M2). The more massive star has the smaller orbit.

Kepler’s third law for binaries relates the orbital period P to the semi-major axis a and total mass:

P2 = 4π2a3 / [G(M1 + M2)]

This equation is the foundation of stellar mass determination: if both P and a can be measured, the total mass follows directly.

Radial Velocity and the Mass Function

In a spectroscopic binary, the orbital motion of each star produces periodic Doppler shifts in its spectral lines. The radial velocity semi-amplitude of star 1 is:

K1 = (2πa1 sin i) / (P√(1 − e2))

where i is the orbital inclination and e the eccentricity. For a single-lined system (SB1), only K1 is measurable, yielding the mass function:

f(M) = M23 sin3(i) / (M1 + M2)2 = PK13(1 − e2)3/2 / (2πG)

The mass function provides a strict lower limit on the companion mass M2. In a double-lined system (SB2), both K1 and K2 are measured, and the mass ratio follows directly: q = M1 / M2 = K2 / K1. If the system also eclipses, the inclination is constrained to be near 90°, removing the sin(i) degeneracy and yielding absolute masses and radii.

Eclipsing Binaries

Eclipsing binaries are systems whose orbital plane is oriented nearly edge-on to the observer, so that each star periodically passes in front of the other. The resulting light curve shows two dips per orbit: a deep primary eclipse when the hotter, brighter star is blocked, and a shallower secondary eclipse when the cooler star is blocked. The depth of each eclipse depends on the surface brightness ratio of the two stars (proportional to T4 for blackbody emitters).

Eclipsing binaries are the primary method for determining stellar masses and radii to precisions better than 1–2%. These fundamental data anchor the calibration of stellar evolution models. The critical inclination for eclipses to occur is icrit = arcsin[(R1 + R2) / a], which for widely separated stars requires near-perfect edge-on alignment.

The Roche Model and Mass Transfer

In close binary systems, the gravitational potential is shaped by both stars and the centrifugal effect of the orbital motion. The resulting Roche potential defines a teardrop-shaped volume around each star called the Roche lobe. If a star expands to fill its Roche lobe — through stellar evolution, for example — material spills through the inner Lagrangian point (L1) onto the companion, a process known as Roche-lobe overflow.

The effective radius of the Roche lobe is well approximated by the Eggleton (1983) formula:

rL / a = 0.49 q2/3 / (0.6 q2/3 + ln(1 + q1/3))

where q = Mdonor / Maccretor. Mass transfer profoundly alters the evolution of both stars, producing exotic objects such as blue stragglers, X-ray binaries, millisecond pulsars, and Type Ia supernovae.

Famous Binary Systems

Algol (β Persei) is the prototype eclipsing binary, known since antiquity for its periodic dimming every 2.867 days. The system consists of a 3.17 M☉ B8 V primary and a 0.70 M☉ K0 IV secondary, viewed at an inclination of ~98.7° with near-zero eccentricity. The primary eclipse dims the system from visual magnitude 2.12 to 3.4 — a drop of ~1.3 mag. Algol also illustrates the Algol paradox: the less massive star is the more evolved subgiant, explained by past mass transfer from the originally more massive component.

Sirius (α Canis Majoris) is a visual binary with a 50.1-year period, comprising Sirius A (2.06 M☉, spectral type A1 V) and Sirius B (1.02 M☉), one of the first white dwarfs discovered.

The Hulse–Taylor binary pulsar (PSR B1913+16), discovered in 1974, provided the first indirect evidence for gravitational waves. The measured orbital decay matches the prediction of general relativity to within 0.2%, earning Russell Hulse and Joseph Taylor the 1993 Nobel Prize in Physics.

Relativistic Binaries

Some binary systems require Einstein’s general theory of relativity to describe their dynamics. Binary pulsars exhibit relativistic effects including the advance of periapsis, gravitational redshift, and orbital decay due to gravitational-wave emission. Compact binaries consisting of neutron stars or black holes that inspiral and merge are the primary sources detected by gravitational-wave observatories such as LIGO and Virgo.
