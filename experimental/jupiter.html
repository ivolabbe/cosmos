Jupiter

Jupiter is the fifth planet from the Sun and the largest in the Solar System. It orbits at a mean distance of 7.78 × 108 km (5.20 AU). A gas giant with a mass 317.8 times that of the Earth, Jupiter contains more than twice the mass of all other planets combined. It has 95 known moons, including the four large Galilean satellites discovered by Galileo Galilei in 1610.

Atmosphere
Jupiter’s atmosphere is composed primarily of molecular hydrogen (~90% by number) and helium (~10%), with trace amounts of methane, ammonia and water vapour. The visible cloud deck is organised into alternating light-coloured zones and darker belts, driven by powerful east–west winds that reach speeds exceeding 600 km/h. The most prominent atmospheric feature is the Great Red Spot, an anticyclonic storm larger than the Earth that has been observed continuously since the 1600s. Data from the Juno mission indicate that the storm extends several hundred kilometres below the cloud tops.

Internal structure
Jupiter has no solid surface. Below the cloud layers, increasing pressure and temperature cause the hydrogen gas to transition smoothly into a liquid state. At depths of roughly 20,000 km, pressures exceed ~2 million atmospheres and hydrogen becomes liquid metallic hydrogen — a state in which electrons are free to move, making it an electrical conductor. This metallic layer is responsible for generating Jupiter’s powerful magnetic field. At the centre lies a possible rocky–icy core with a mass of approximately 10 Earth masses, though recent data are consistent with a dilute core that may extend over a significant fraction of the planet’s radius. Jupiter’s equatorial diameter is 142,984 km and its mean density is just 1.33 g/cm³, lower than any of the terrestrial planets.

Rotation and magnetosphere
Jupiter has the fastest rotation of any planet in the Solar System, completing one turn in just 9 hours 56 minutes. This rapid spin produces a noticeable equatorial bulging (oblateness of ~0.065), meaning the equatorial diameter exceeds the polar diameter by approximately 9,300 km. The rotation, combined with convective motions in the liquid metallic hydrogen layer, drives a magnetosphere roughly 20,000 times stronger than Earth’s. Jupiter’s magnetosphere is the largest structure in the Solar System, with a tail extending beyond the orbit of Saturn.

Galilean moons
The four largest moons of Jupiter — Io, Europa, Ganymede and Callisto — were discovered by Galileo in 1610 and provided early evidence that not all celestial bodies orbit the Earth.

  • Io is the most volcanically active body in the Solar System, with hundreds of volcanoes driven by intense tidal heating from Jupiter’s gravity.
  • Europa possesses a smooth, icy surface beneath which lies a global subsurface ocean of liquid water, making it one of the most compelling targets in the search for extraterrestrial life (astrobiology).
  • Ganymede is the largest moon in the Solar System — larger even than the planet Mercury — and the only moon known to generate its own magnetic field.
  • Callisto is heavily cratered and geologically inactive, with a surface that records billions of years of impact history.

Role in the Solar System
Jupiter’s enormous gravitational influence shapes the architecture of the Solar System. Its gravity captures or deflects comets and asteroids that might otherwise reach the inner planets, a role sometimes described as a “cosmic shield.” Jupiter also sculpts the asteroid belt through orbital resonances — particularly the Kirkwood gaps — where its periodic gravitational perturbations clear out asteroids at specific semi-major axes. During the early history of the Solar System, Jupiter’s migration likely influenced the formation and delivery of volatile materials to the terrestrial planets.
