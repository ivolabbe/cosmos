Pulsar

Jocelyn Bell (the discoverer of the first radio pulsar) with the antenna that was used.
Credit: © J. Bell-Burnell

A pulsar is an extraterrestrial source of radiation that has a regular periodicity, usually detected in the form of short bursts of radio emission. A typical pulsar is a rapidly rotating neutron star with a mass of roughly 1.4 M☉, a radius of only about 10 km, and a density of ~1014 g/cm3. Most of the known pulsars are only visible in the radio region of the electromagnetic spectrum and are called radio pulsars, but a small number emit at optical wavelengths, X-ray wavelengths, and gamma-ray wavelengths. The first radio pulsar was discovered in 1967 by Jocelyn Bell, a graduate student from Glasgow University working with Antony Hewish at the Mullard Radio Astronomy Observatory in Cambridge. Hewish received the 1974 Nobel Prize in Physics for his ‘decisive role in the discovery of pulsars’.

The lighthouse model
Radio pulsars are generally accepted to be highly magnetised, rapidly rotating neutron stars with a lighthouse beam of radiation that produces the pulsed emission. The magnetic field axis is tilted with respect to the rotation axis, so that beams of electromagnetic radiation emitted along the magnetic poles sweep through space as the star rotates. An observer whose line of sight intersects the beam sees a periodic flash — the “pulse.” The angular radius of the emission cone scales with period as ρ ≈ 5.75° × P−1/2 (where P is in seconds), giving a typical duty cycle of roughly 5%.

Normal radio pulsars have surface magnetic fields of order 1012 G. Millisecond pulsars — old neutron stars that have been spun up by accretion from a companion star — have weaker fields of order 108 G but spin periods below ~30 ms. The fastest known pulsar is PSR J1748–2446ad, rotating at 716 Hz (a period of just 1.396 ms). Observed spin periods range from 1.4 ms up to 8.5 s.

Some radio pulsars are associated with supernova remnants, and it is generally accepted that pulsars are the collapsed cores of stars that were once more massive than 6–10 times the mass of the Sun. As pulsars radiate energy, their rotation frequency gradually slows and they eventually cease radiating altogether. The rate of slow-down is characterised by the period derivative Ṗ, and an estimate of the pulsar’s age is given by the characteristic age τc = P / (2Ṗ). The surface magnetic field strength can be inferred from the same observables: B > 3.2 × 1019 × (P Ṗ)1/2 G. The associated spin-down luminosity — the rate at which rotational kinetic energy is lost — is given by Ė = 4π2IṖ/P3, where I ≈ 1045 g cm2 is the moment of inertia. For the Crab pulsar (P = 33.1 ms, Ṗ ≈ 4.2 × 10−13 s/s), this yields a spin-down power of ~4 × 1038 erg/s — roughly 105 times the luminosity of the Sun.

X-ray pulsars
X-ray pulsars emit X-rays at regular intervals, either because of magnetospheric emission in neutron stars, or through the accretion of matter from a companion. Accretion-powered X-ray sources with strong magnetic fields usually pulse relatively slowly (some as slowly as once every 20 minutes), as the magnetic field exerts a slow-down torque on the neutron star due to the presence of ionised material. Some X-ray pulsars rotate very rapidly, with spin frequencies in excess of 400 Hz. These millisecond X-ray pulsars are the precursors of the millisecond radio pulsars. The luminosity of X-ray pulsars varies over at least five orders of magnitude, from near the Eddington limit of 1031 J s−1 to less than 1026 J s−1. Different classes of X-ray pulsars exist:

  • High-mass X-ray binaries (HMXBs) are usually powered by a strong stellar wind emanating from a massive companion star (5–30 times as massive as the neutron star).
  • Low-mass X-ray binaries (LMXBs) are fed by mass transfer through Roche-lobe overflow, and have donor stars that are less massive than the neutron star. The mass transfer widens the orbit, making LMXBs long-lived sources compared to HMXBs.

Optical pulsars
Optical pulsars form a very small subset of known pulsars. The most famous optical pulsar is the Crab pulsar, the remnant of a supernova explosion that was visible in 1054 AD (shown in the images on the right).

Gamma-ray pulsars
Gamma-ray pulsars are quite rare, and most are young neutron stars with strong magnetic fields. A subset of these are visible as radio and optical pulsars, and the enigmatic Geminga has no detectable radio emission despite very deep searches. At gamma-ray wavelengths near 100 MeV, the Vela pulsar (P ≈ 89 ms) is the strongest point source in the sky.

Magnetars
A small population of neutron stars possess extraordinarily strong magnetic fields of 1014–1015 G — roughly a thousand times stronger than ordinary pulsars. These magnetars include the soft gamma repeaters (SGRs) and anomalous X-ray pulsars (AXPs). Their emission is thought to be powered primarily by the decay of the intense magnetic field rather than by rotational energy loss.

Pulsar census
As of 2024, the number of known radio pulsars exceeds 3,500, catalogued by the ATNF Pulsar Catalogue. Pulsar timing data are used for a remarkable range of applications: testing general relativity in strong-field regimes, detecting gravitational waves via pulsar timing arrays, constraining the equation of state of ultra-dense matter, and probing the interstellar medium through dispersion measure variations.
