Rotation Curve

A rotation curve is a plot showing how orbital velocity v varies with distance R from the centre of a rotating system. In astronomy, rotation curves are used to map the distribution of mass within the Solar System (where they follow Keplerian motion), within spiral galaxies, and within galaxy clusters. The observed rotation curves of spiral galaxies remain approximately flat at large radii — far beyond the visible stellar disk — providing one of the strongest pieces of evidence for dark matter.

The Rotation Curve Problem

If the mass of a spiral galaxy were concentrated in the visible stars and gas, the orbital velocities of material at large galactocentric radii should decline as v ∝ R−1/2, following Kepler’s laws — just as the outer planets of the Solar System orbit more slowly than the inner ones. Instead, observations show that velocities remain approximately constant (flat) far beyond the visible disk, typically at 150–300 km/s. This discrepancy between the expected Keplerian decline and the observed flat curve is known as the rotation curve problem, and it implies that a massive, invisible halo of dark matter surrounds every spiral galaxy.

The problem was first identified by Babcock (1939), who noted unexpectedly high velocities in the outer regions of the Andromeda Galaxy (M31). It was Vera Rubin and Kent Ford, through systematic observations of dozens of spiral galaxies in the 1970s and 1980s, who established that flat rotation curves are universal. Their work demonstrated that visible matter accounts for only a fraction of the total mass of galaxies, providing some of the most compelling evidence for the existence of dark matter (Rubin & Ford 1970; Rubin, Ford & Thonnard 1980).

Measuring Rotation Curves

The rotation curves of spiral galaxies are measured using two complementary techniques. At radio wavelengths, the 21 cm emission line of neutral hydrogen (Hi) traces gas out to large radii, often extending well beyond the visible stellar disk. At optical wavelengths, the Hα emission line from ionised gas in H II regions provides high spatial resolution measurements of the inner disk. The Doppler shift of these spectral lines along the major axis of an inclined galaxy yields the line-of-sight component of the circular velocity at each radius.

By equating gravitational and centripetal forces for circular orbits, the enclosed mass within a radius R can be estimated:

v2 = GM(R) / R

where v is the circular velocity, G is Newton’s gravitational constant, and M(R) is the total mass enclosed within radius R. A flat rotation curve (v ≈ constant) therefore implies that M(R) grows linearly with R at large radii, requiring a density profile that falls as ρ ∝ r−2 — the hallmark of an isothermal sphere.

The modern standard dataset is the SPARC database (Lelli, McGaugh & Schombert 2016, AJ 152, 157), which contains high-quality rotation curves for 175 disk galaxies measured from a combination of Hi and Hα data.

Mass Model Decomposition

The rotation curve of a spiral galaxy can be decomposed into contributions from three major components: the central bulge, the exponential disk, and the dark matter halo. Because these components have different radial density profiles, each dominates the gravitational potential at different radii. The total circular velocity at radius R is obtained by adding the individual contributions in quadrature:

vtotal(R) = √(vbulge2 + vdisk2 + vhalo2)

Bulge. The central stellar bulge is a compact, roughly spheroidal concentration of old stars. Its mass distribution is commonly described by the Hernquist (1990) profile, which yields a circular velocity that rises steeply at small radii, peaks near the bulge scale radius ab, and then declines. In the Milky Way, the bulge mass is approximately 1.5–1.8 × 1010 M☉ with a scale radius of about 0.5 kpc (Sofue 2009).

Disk. The exponential disk follows the Freeman (1970) surface density profile, Σ(R) = Σ0 exp(−R/Rd), where Rd is the disk scale length. The corresponding circular velocity involves modified Bessel functions and peaks at R ≈ 2.2 Rd. For the Milky Way, Rd ≈ 2.6 kpc (Bland-Hawthorn & Gerhard 2016) and the disk mass is approximately 5 × 1010 M☉. The disk component peaks near 5–7 kpc and then declines gently.

Dark matter halo. The dark matter halo is the dominant mass component at large radii. Two common models are the NFW profile (Navarro, Frenk & White 1996), which has a central density cusp (ρ ∝ r−1), and the pseudo-isothermal sphere (Begeman, Broeils & Sanders 1991), which has a constant-density core. The pseudo-isothermal halo produces a velocity that rises from zero, flattens at the core radius Rc, and approaches a constant asymptotic value v∞ at large radii:

vhalo(R) = v∞ √(1 − (Rc/R) arctan(R/Rc))

In the Milky Way, the halo has an NFW concentration parameter c ≈ 10–15, and the local dark matter density near the Sun is approximately 0.3 GeV/cm3 (≈ 0.01 M☉/pc3).

The Milky Way Rotation Curve

The Milky Way rotation curve has been constructed from a variety of tracers, including Hi emission, CO molecular line surveys, Cepheid variables, and maser sources. The data show a peak near R ≈ 0.3–0.5 kpc associated with the bulge, a mild dip, and then approximately flat behaviour from about 5 kpc outward. The IAU standard value for the circular velocity at the solar galactocentric distance (R☉ ≈ 8.2 kpc; Gravity Collaboration 2019) is vc = 220 km/s (Sofue 2009), although recent measurements suggest a value closer to 230–240 km/s.

The flat rotation curve beyond ~5 kpc persists out to at least 25 kpc, and Hi data from external galaxies extend to 50 kpc or more. These data are among the most robust evidence that dark matter constitutes a far greater fraction of a galaxy’s total mass than the visible baryonic matter. In the Milky Way, the dark halo is estimated to contain roughly 5–10 times the mass of the visible disk and bulge combined.

Reference Rotation Curve Types

Three idealised rotation curve shapes serve as useful references for comparison with observations:

Keplerian. When all mass is concentrated at or near the centre (as in the Solar System), the circular velocity declines as v(R) = √(GM/R) ∝ R−1/2. This is the expected behaviour for a spiral galaxy if no dark matter halo were present.

Solid body. If mass is uniformly distributed (constant density), the velocity increases linearly with radius: v(R) ∝ R. This behaviour is seen in the innermost regions of many galaxies, where the mass density is roughly uniform.

Flat. An approximately constant velocity at all radii, v(R) ≈ constant, is the hallmark of spiral galaxy rotation curves beyond the inner few kiloparsecs. A flat curve implies M(R) ∝ R, consistent with a massive dark matter halo.
