Neptune

Neptune is the eighth and most distant planet from the Sun, orbiting at a mean distance of 4.50 × 109 km (30.07 AU). It is an ice giant with a mass 17.1 times that of Earth, 16 known moons, and a faint ring system. Neptune was discovered in 1846 through mathematical prediction — a landmark triumph of celestial mechanics.

Discovery
Irregularities in the orbit of Uranus led two mathematicians — John Couch Adams in England and Urbain Le Verrier in France — to independently predict the existence and position of an unseen planet. Using Le Verrier’s calculations, Johann Galle at the Berlin Observatory found Neptune on 23 September 1846, less than one degree from the predicted position. The discovery demonstrated that Newtonian gravity could be used not merely to describe known motions but to reveal previously unknown bodies — a profound triumph of celestial mechanics.

Atmosphere
Neptune’s atmosphere is composed primarily of hydrogen and helium, with a small but significant fraction of methane. Methane absorbs red wavelengths of light, giving the planet its vivid blue colour. Atmospheric data indicate that Neptune hosts the strongest sustained winds in the Solar System, reaching up to 2,100 km/h. Voyager 2 observed a large anticyclonic storm dubbed the Great Dark Spot in 1989, but subsequent Hubble Space Telescope observations showed the feature had disappeared, demonstrating how rapidly Neptune’s weather systems evolve.

Internal structure
Neptune is classified as an ice giant, distinct from the gas giants Jupiter and Saturn. Beneath the outer gaseous envelope lies a thick mantle of water, methane and ammonia ices compressed to high densities and temperatures. At the centre sits a dense core of rock and ice. Neptune has an equatorial diameter of 49,528 km and a mean density of 1.64 g/cm³.

Moons and orbital period
The largest of Neptune’s moons is Triton, which follows a retrograde orbit — strongly suggesting it is a captured Kuiper Belt object. Voyager 2 imaged active geysers of nitrogen gas erupting from Triton’s surface, where temperatures fall to approximately −235 °C, making it one of the coldest known surfaces in the Solar System. Neptune’s orbital period is 164.8 years; it completed its first full orbit since discovery in 2011.

Exploration
Neptune has been visited by only one spacecraft: Voyager 2, which made a close flyby on 25 August 1989. The encounter provided the first detailed data on the planet’s atmosphere, ring arcs, magnetic field and moons. No dedicated orbiter mission to Neptune has yet been launched.
