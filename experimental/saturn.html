Saturn

Saturn is the sixth planet from the Sun, orbiting at a mean distance of 1.43 × 109 km (9.58 AU). It is the second largest planet in the Solar System, after Jupiter, and is famous for its extensive ring system, visible even through a small telescope. Saturn has 146 known moons.

Ring system
Saturn’s rings are composed primarily of water ice particles and rocky debris, ranging in size from centimetres to several metres. The ring system spans approximately 280,000 km in diameter but is remarkably thin — only about 10 m thick. The main rings are designated A, B and C (the C ring is also known as the Crepe Ring). The prominent Cassini Division, a 4,800 km gap between the A and B rings, is maintained by orbital resonance with the moon Mimas. Data from the Cassini mission indicate that the rings are probably young, having formed only about 100 million years ago — far younger than Saturn itself.

Structure
Saturn is a gas giant composed predominantly of hydrogen and helium. It has an equatorial diameter of 120,536 km and a mass of 95.2 Earth masses. Its mean density is just 0.687 g/cm³ — less than that of water. Saturn is the only planet in the Solar System that would, in principle, float if placed in a sufficiently large body of water.

Atmosphere
The atmosphere is approximately 96% hydrogen and 3% helium by volume, with traces of ammonia, methane and water vapour. Ammonia crystals in the upper cloud layers give Saturn its pale gold appearance. Wind speeds in the equatorial region reach up to 1,800 km/h, among the fastest in the Solar System. At Saturn’s north pole, a persistent hexagonal vortex — roughly 30,000 km across — was first observed by Voyager and later studied in detail by Cassini. The data gathered on this structure are still being analysed to understand the atmospheric dynamics that sustain it.

Moons
Saturn’s 146 known moons include several of exceptional scientific interest. Titan, the largest, has a thick nitrogen-dominated atmosphere with a surface pressure 1.5 times that of Earth. Radar data from Cassini revealed lakes and seas of liquid methane and ethane on its surface. Titan is larger than Mercury. Enceladus harbours a global subsurface ocean beneath its icy crust; Cassini flew through water-rich jets erupting from its south polar region and detected hydrogen and organic molecules in the plume material, making Enceladus a prime target for astrobiology research. Other notable moons include Mimas, whose large Herschel crater gives it a distinctive appearance, and Iapetus, which has a dramatic two-tone surface — one hemisphere bright and the other dark.

Exploration
Saturn has been visited by four spacecraft. Pioneer 11 made the first flyby in 1979, followed by Voyager 1 in 1980 and Voyager 2 in 1981. The most comprehensive data were returned by the Cassini–Huygens mission, a joint endeavour of NASA, the European Space Agency and the Italian Space Agency. Cassini entered orbit around Saturn in 2004 and operated for 13 years, returning a wealth of data on the planet, its rings and its moons. In January 2005, the Huygens probe separated from Cassini and descended through Titan’s atmosphere, landing on its surface — the most distant landing ever achieved by a human-made craft. Cassini’s mission concluded in September 2017 with a deliberate plunge into Saturn’s atmosphere.
