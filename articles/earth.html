Earth

Earth is the third planet from the Sun, orbiting at a mean distance of 1.50 × 108 km (1.00 AU). It is the only planet known to harbour life, the only world in the Solar System with stable liquid water on its surface, and the densest of all the planets. Earth has one natural satellite, the Moon.

Atmosphere
Earth’s atmosphere is composed primarily of nitrogen (78%) and oxygen (21%), with trace amounts of argon, carbon dioxide, water vapour and other gases. The greenhouse effect — driven chiefly by water vapour, carbon dioxide, and methane — raises the mean surface temperature from roughly −18 °C (the bare radiative equilibrium value) to about +15 °C, maintaining conditions suitable for liquid water. An ozone layer in the stratosphere absorbs the majority of incoming solar ultraviolet radiation, shielding the surface and enabling complex life. Dynamic weather systems — driven by differential solar heating, the Coriolis effect, and latent heat release — redistribute energy from the equator toward the poles.

Structure
Earth has an equatorial diameter of 12,742 km, a mass of 5.97 × 1024 kg, and a mean density of 5.51 g/cm3 — the highest of any planet in the Solar System. Its interior is differentiated into a solid iron–nickel inner core, a liquid iron–nickel outer core, a thick silicate mantle, and a thin rocky crust. Convection currents in the outer core generate Earth’s global magnetic field through a self-sustaining dynamo mechanism. The mantle convects on timescales of tens of millions of years, driving plate tectonics — the slow motion and recycling of lithospheric plates that shapes the surface through volcanism, mountain building, and seafloor spreading.

Axial tilt and seasons
Earth’s rotational axis is tilted at 23.44° relative to the perpendicular of its orbital plane. This obliquity is the primary cause of the seasons: as Earth orbits the Sun, the Northern and Southern Hemispheres alternate between receiving more and less direct solar radiation. Earth completes one rotation on its axis in 23 h 56 m (one sidereal day) and one orbit around the Sun in 365.25 days (one sidereal year), which necessitates the insertion of a leap day every four years to keep the calendar aligned with the seasons.

Water and life
Approximately 71% of Earth’s surface is covered by oceans, with a total water volume of about 1.335 × 109 km3. The water cycle — evaporation, condensation, precipitation and runoff — continuously redistributes water between the oceans, atmosphere, and land. Earth occupies the habitable zone (also called the Goldilocks zone) around the Sun, the region where surface temperatures permit liquid water to exist. The data from decades of exoplanet surveys indicate that rocky worlds in the habitable zones of their host stars are common, but Earth remains the only body where life has been confirmed. Earth’s magnetic field plays a critical role in habitability: it deflects the solar wind and shields the atmosphere from erosion by charged particles, preserving the air and water that sustain life.

The Moon
Earth’s sole natural satellite, the Moon, has a diameter of 3,474 km — about one-quarter that of Earth — making the Earth–Moon system unusual among the terrestrial planets for the large relative size of the satellite. The Moon is thought to have formed from debris ejected by a giant impact between the proto-Earth and a Mars-sized body roughly 4.5 billion years ago. Its gravitational influence stabilises Earth’s axial tilt, preventing large chaotic oscillations in obliquity that could otherwise produce extreme climatic swings. Tidal interactions between Earth and the Moon raise ocean tides and gradually slow Earth’s rotation, lengthening the day by approximately 2.3 milliseconds per century.
