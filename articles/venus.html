Venus

Venus is the second planet from the Sun with an average distance to the Sun of 1.08 × 108 km or 0.72 AU. It is an inferior planet in that its orbit lies inside that of Earth, and it never appears more than 47° from the Sun in the sky. Often called Earth’s “sister planet” because of its similar size, Venus is the brightest natural object in the night sky after the Moon, easily visible to the naked eye as the “morning star” or “evening star.” It has no known natural satellites.

Atmosphere
Venus possesses the densest atmosphere of any terrestrial planet. It is composed almost entirely of carbon dioxide (96.5%), with roughly 3.5% nitrogen and trace amounts of sulphur dioxide, water vapour and other gases. The surface pressure is approximately 92 atmospheres — equivalent to the pressure at a depth of nearly 900 m in Earth’s oceans. This massive CO2 envelope drives a runaway greenhouse effect that raises the surface temperature to approximately 465°C, making Venus the hottest planet in the Solar System — hotter even than Mercury, despite being nearly twice as far from the Sun. The upper atmosphere is shrouded in thick clouds of sulphuric acid droplets that completely obscure the surface at visible wavelengths and reflect about 75% of incoming sunlight, giving Venus its high albedo.

Rotation
Venus rotates on its axis in the retrograde direction — that is, opposite to the sense in which most planets rotate and orbit the Sun. Its sidereal rotation period is 243 Earth days, longer than its orbital period of 224.7 Earth days. A Venusian day (from sunrise to sunrise) is therefore about 117 Earth days. The cause of this slow, retrograde spin is not fully understood; hypotheses include tidal effects from the dense atmosphere, a large ancient impact, or gravitational interactions over the age of the Solar System. The axial tilt is 177°, meaning Venus is nearly upside-down relative to its orbital plane.

Surface
The surface of Venus is hidden beneath its opaque cloud deck and has been mapped primarily by radar. The most detailed global survey was conducted by NASA’s Magellan spacecraft between 1990 and 1994, which imaged approximately 98% of the surface at resolutions better than 100 m. These data reveal a landscape dominated by volcanic plains, with over 1,600 major volcanoes and numerous shield volcanoes, lava flows and pancake domes. Two main highland regions stand above the plains: Ishtar Terra in the north, roughly the size of Australia, and the larger Aphrodite Terra near the equator. The highest point on Venus is Maxwell Montes, located in Ishtar Terra, rising approximately 11 km above the mean surface level. Impact craters are distributed nearly uniformly across the surface, suggesting that a global volcanic resurfacing event occurred roughly 300–600 million years ago.

Size and structure
Venus has a diameter of 12,104 km (0.95 Earth diameters) and a mass of 4.87 × 1024 kg, or 0.815 times that of Earth. Its mean density is 5.24 g cm-3, slightly lower than Earth’s 5.51 g cm-3. Internal structure models suggest an iron core similar in size to Earth’s, a silicate mantle and a thin crust. However, Venus has no measurable global magnetic field. The absence of a dynamo-driven field may be related to the extremely slow rotation or to the lack of an inner solid core, though the precise cause remains an open question.

Exploration
Venus was the first planet to be visited by a spacecraft (Mariner 2, 1962) and the first to have a probe land on its surface. The Soviet Venera programme achieved a series of landmark firsts: Venera 7 (1970) returned the first data from the surface of another planet, and Venera 9 (1975) transmitted the first photographs from another planet’s surface. Subsequent Venera landers survived the extreme conditions long enough to return data on temperature, pressure, soil composition and panoramic images. NASA’s Magellan mission (1990–1994) produced a near-complete radar map of the surface. ESA’s Venus Express (2006–2014) studied the atmosphere and climate in detail, discovering evidence of recent volcanic activity. JAXA’s Akatsuki orbiter, in operation since 2015, continues to monitor atmospheric dynamics. A new generation of missions is planned: NASA’s VERITAS and DAVINCI+ (selected 2021) will map the surface at higher resolution and descend through the atmosphere to measure its composition, while ESA’s EnVision will characterise geological activity and interior structure.
