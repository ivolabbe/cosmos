Uranus

Uranus is the seventh planet from the Sun, orbiting at a mean distance of 2.87×109 km (19.19 AU). It is an ice giant with a mass 14.5 times that of Earth and 27 known moons. Uranus was discovered by William Herschel on 13 March 1781, making it the first planet found with a telescope.

Extreme axial tilt
Uranus has an axial tilt of 97.77°, meaning it rotates essentially on its side — the most extreme tilt of any planet in the Solar System. As a consequence, the polar regions receive more sunlight than the equator over the course of a Uranian year. Each pole experiences roughly 42 Earth years of continuous sunlight followed by roughly 42 Earth years of darkness. The cause of this extreme tilt remains unknown, though it is thought to have resulted from a giant impact early in Solar System history.

Atmosphere
The atmosphere of Uranus is composed primarily of hydrogen (83%), helium (15%) and methane (2%). Methane in the upper atmosphere absorbs red light, giving the planet its distinctive blue-green colour. Uranus possesses the coldest planetary atmosphere in the Solar System, with a minimum temperature of −224°C. In visible light, the atmosphere appears relatively featureless compared to that of Neptune.

Structure
Uranus is classified as an ice giant, a category distinct from the gas giants Jupiter and Saturn. Its interior is dominated by water, methane and ammonia ices above a small rocky core. The planet has a diameter of 51,118 km and a density of 1.27 g/cm³. Its orbital period is 84 Earth years.

Ring system
Uranus has 13 known rings, discovered in 1977 during a stellar occultation. The rings are very dark and narrow compared to the bright, extensive ring system of Saturn.

Moons
Uranus has 27 known moons, all named after characters from the works of William Shakespeare and Alexander Pope. The five major moons are Miranda, Ariel, Umbriel, Titania and Oberon. Miranda is notable for its dramatic cliff Verona Rupes, which at approximately 20 km high is one of the tallest known cliffs in the Solar System.

Exploration
Uranus has been visited by only one spacecraft: Voyager 2, which made its closest approach in January 1986. The data from that brief flyby remain the primary source of close-range observations. A dedicated Uranus orbiter is considered a high-priority mission for future Solar System exploration.
